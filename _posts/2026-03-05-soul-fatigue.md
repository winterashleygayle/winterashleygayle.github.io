Every once in a while life presents a moment that quietly asks you to look at yourself and reconsider what you thought you knew. A pause. A moment where you step back and ask whether the way you are valuing yourself truly matches the value you bring to the table.

Recently I found myself in one of those moments and I’ve been quietly smiling to myself ever since. 

⸻

Last week I had a really interesting conversation with a friend about my career. Things are moving in a positive direction and I recently presented a proposal that involved a 25 percent position in something I am working toward. It felt like a reasonable starting point to me.

But when I mentioned this to a few friends, several of them had the same reaction. They asked why I would begin at 25 percent if I am already contributing significant value. Their perspective was simple: if the work being brought to the table is closer to 50 percent of the effort or responsibility, why not start the conversation there?

That question shifted something in my thinking. It made me pause and examine how we value our contributions. Sometimes we instinctively start small because it feels safer or more modest. But there are moments in life when beginning from a place of confidence and clarity about our value may actually be the more honest position.

Then another close friend took the idea even further. He said he sees me operating at 100 percent and wondered why I was even considering a partnership at all. His point was not necessarily that I should go it alone, but that sometimes we underestimate our own capacity.

These conversations sparked a deeper reflection in me. At one point I said something that surprised both of us. I admitted that a part of me can feel lazy. Not lazy in the sense that I cannot work hard. I have proven to myself many times that I can work long hours, push through fatigue, and carry a heavy workload if necessary.

My friend immediately understood what I meant and said I was speaking her language. The idea that what we often call laziness might be something deeper resonated with her.

Over the past week, I kept thinking about that conversation. The more I reflected on it, the more I realized that what I had called laziness was not laziness at all. It was something closer to nervous system fatigue.

There is a difference between being unwilling to work and being deeply tired at a nervous system level. Many people today are experiencing a kind of quiet exhaustion that is not simply about sleep or productivity. It comes from living in an environment of constant stimulation. We are surrounded by nonstop information, advertising, expectations, and pressure to always be achieving more. The cultural message is often to keep pushing, keep building, keep doing.

But our nervous systems were not designed for endless acceleration.

Sometimes the quiet resistance we feel is actually our nervous system trying to restore balance. It is the body asking for space to think clearly rather than reacting to the constant noise of the outside world.

That realization has been surprisingly grounding for me. Instead of forcing an immediate answer about whether something should be 25 percent, 50 percent, or even 100 percent, I am allowing myself to pause. Slowing down creates the space to ask better questions.

What actually feels aligned with my values?
What pace of work supports long term sustainability rather than burnout?
What decision feels right not only intellectually, but physiologically?

Listening to the nervous system requires a different kind of attention. It asks us to step out of the constant pressure to perform and instead become curious about the quieter signals within us.

For me, the most important realization right now is that I do not have to rush the answer. The deeper parts of our intuition often speak more softly than the louder voices of ambition, comparison, or external expectation. But when we slow down enough to hear them, they can guide us toward decisions that are both successful and sustainable.

In a world that constantly encourages us to do more, there is real wisdom in learning when to pause, reflect, and listen.  

What I am learning is that clarity does not always come from pushing harder or deciding faster. Sometimes it comes from allowing space. From listening to the quieter voice inside that is not competing with the noise of the outside world.

For now I am comfortable sitting in that pause. I can look at myself honestly, reflect on what I am learning, and smile at the recognition that I do not need the answer immediately. Sometimes the most important step forward is simply giving yourself permission to listen.
